Call 999 now if:

  • you’ve hurt your head badly, eg deep wound from a fall or accident
  • your headache came on suddenly and is very painful (like being hit with a brick)
  • you have a sudden headache after: coughing, laughing, sneezing, changing posture, exercise — a blood vessel might have burst in your brain
  • you suddenly have problems speaking, can’t remember things, feel drowsy, confused
  • you have other symptoms like: high fever, stiff neck, a rash, jaw pain when chewing, vision problems, redness in one of your eyes

What you can do about heachaches

Most headaches will go away after a few hours. They aren’t a sign of something more serious.

You can simply wait until the headache goes away or get painkillers like paracetamol or ibuprofen at the pharmacy.

Don’t take more painkillers than it says on the package. Taking them too often can make your headache worse.

This will also help with your headache:

  • drink enough water
  • eat regular meals
  • roughly sleep the same amount of hours every night — too much or too little sleep can cause headaches
  • avoid things that stress you where possible

Check what kind of headache you have

Whole head aching and tight feeling

Normal headaches feel like your whole head is aching and has a tight band wrapped around it. They can last between 30 minutes and several hours.

They’re also called “tension headaches”.

You can get them, for example, because of:

  • not eating or drinking enough water
  • stress
  • bad posture
  • cold and flu
  • inflamed sinuses — you’ll feel sharp pain behind the root of your nose or below both eyes

To treat them, check above what you can do about headaches.

Bad pain and feeling sick

A very bad, throbbing at the front or side of the head can be a migraine.

You might also feel sick, vomit and find light or noise painful. Some migraines start with seeing flashing lights.

Check if you have any symptoms that might be an emergency.

The Migraine Trust has information about types or migraine and treatments.

See your GP for bad migraines.

Headache attacks at certain times of the year

Some headaches come in attacks at a certain time of year. They’re very painful. You might also have bad pain around one eye, watering or red eyes and a runny nose.

These are called “cluster headaches” and are much less common than everyday headaches.

You should see your GP if you have these kinds of attacks.

Taking the pill, period or menopause

Some women also get headaches when they’re on the pill or during their period or menopause.

To treat them, check above what you can do about headaches.
