Many illnesses in children cause a rash.  Even though some may take a while to clear up, many rashes in children are nothing to worry about

Call 999 or go to your nearest A&E if your child has a rash and they

  • have a stiff neck
  • are bothered by light
  • seem confused
  • are having a fit
  • have a fever you can’t control
  • have a rash that doesn’t fade when you press a glass against it

Rash with no other symptoms

White spots in babies

Small white spots (milia) often appear on a baby’s face when they are a few days old. They usually clear up within a few weeks and don’t need treatment.  

Raised red, yellow and white spots in babies

Raised red yellow and white spots can appear on babies within a few hours of them being born. They’re usually on the face, in the middle of the body and the upper arms and thighs.

The rash can disappear and reappear. It’s known as erythema toxicum

It should clear up in a week or two without treatment.

Red and yellow spots on baby’s face

Raised, red and yellow spots often appear on a baby’s face when they are a few weeks old.  This is known as baby acne.

It usually goes away after a few weeks and doesn’t need treatment.  

Speak to your GP if it lasts longer than 3 months.

Pink or skin coloured spots

Small, firm, raised spots that can appear anywhere on the body are common in children and known as molluscum contagiosum

Treatment isn’t recommended as the spots clear up, although it can take over a year. It’s infectious so you should avoid squeezing or scratching the spots and keep them covered with clothing if possible. 

Nappy rash

Nappy rash can be red patches on your baby’s bottom, or the whole nappy area.  The skin may look sore and feel hot. There may be spots or blisters. Your child may feel uncomfortable or distressed if the rash is bad.

You can buy creams from your pharmacy to help clear it up.  You should also change the nappy regularly and as soon as your baby has done a poo.

Rash with no other symptoms

Rash caused by heat

Heat and sweat can cause small, red spots known as prickly heat or heat rash. They are itchy so you may notice your baby scratching.

This should clear up without treatment.  A cool bath and loose cotton clothes may help.

Itchy, red, scaly skin

Skin which is itchy, red, dry and cracked may be eczema. It usually affects skin where it folds, such as behind the knees and elbows and the neck, but can appear anywhere.

Speak to your GP if you think your child has eczema.

Raised, itchy rash

A raised, red, itchy rash (hives) can appear as an allergic reaction to things like stings, medicines or food.

It usually clears up within a few days.  

Speak to your GP if your child keeps getting rashes. They may be allergic to something.

Itchy, round rash

An itchy, ring-like rash can be ringworm

Ask your pharmacist for a cream or lotion to treat ringworm.

If it appears on your child’s scalp, speak to your GP, as it may need to be treated with medicine.

Rash with fever

Fever and red cheeks

A fever with bright red rash on both cheeks can be slapped cheek syndrome. It may seem like your child has a cold and the rash may spread to the body.

It usually clears up within a week.  Children’s paracetamol can be taken to control the fever.

Small spots and blisters

Chickenpox causes red spots that turn to blisters.  They can be itchy.  They eventually scab and fall off.  Some children have a few spots, while others have them all over their body.

Chickenpox can be identified and treated at home, without seeing your GP. Call your GP if you’re not sure what the rash is.

Blisters on hands and feet and in the mouth.

A common childhood illness that causes blisters on the hands and feet and ulcers on the tongue, is hand, foot and mouth disease.  It also causes a fever and your child may seem like they have a cold.

Pink, red rash

Scarlet fever causes a pink-red rash, which feels like sandpaper and looks like sunburn (although it’s not awlays in areas exposed to the sun).

It usually starts with a sore throat, headache, fever and a swollen tongue.

See your GP as soon as possible if you suspect scarlet fever. It’s treated with antibiotics.
